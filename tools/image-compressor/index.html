How this image compressor works

Your images are drawn onto an HTML5 Canvas element at the quality level you set, then re-encoded using your browser's built-in JPEG or WebP codec. The entire process happens on your device — image data never leaves your browser. This makes it safe for confidential documents, medical images, legal photos, or any file you wouldn't upload to a third-party server.

JPEG vs WebP vs PNG — which to choose?

Auto keeps the original format but compresses it. WebP consistently produces the smallest files (25–35% smaller than JPEG at equal quality) and is supported by all modern browsers — ideal for websites. JPEG is universally compatible and best for photographs. PNG is lossless and best for screenshots, logos, and images with transparency — compressing PNG reduces metadata and unused palette entries rather than image data.

What quality setting should I use?

For most web images, 75–85% quality produces files that are indistinguishable from the original at normal viewing distances, while being 40–70% smaller. Going below 60% introduces visible artifacts. The 82% default is a good starting point for most use cases. For print-quality output, stay above 90%.
