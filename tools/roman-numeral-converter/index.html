How Roman numerals work

Roman numerals use seven letters: I (1), V (5), X (10), L (50), C (100), D (500), and M (1000). When a smaller value appears before a larger one, it is subtracted rather than added — so IV means 4 (5−1), and IX means 9 (10−1). The six subtractive combinations are: IV (4), IX (9), XL (40), XC (90), CD (400), and CM (900). All other combinations are additive, reading left to right from largest to smallest.

Where are Roman numerals used today?

Roman numerals are still widely used for clock faces, chapter numbers in books, movie sequel titles, the Super Bowl (Super Bowl LVII), copyright years on TV and film productions, and the names of monarchs and popes (Elizabeth II, Pope John Paul II). The current year is a common conversion — in 2024, for example, we are in MMXXIV.
