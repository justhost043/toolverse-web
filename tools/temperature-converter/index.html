Temperature scale history

Celsius was defined by Swedish astronomer Anders Celsius in 1742, originally with 0° as the boiling point and 100° as freezing — later reversed to the familiar scale. Fahrenheit was created by German physicist Daniel Fahrenheit in 1724; 32°F is the freezing point of water and 212°F is the boiling point. Kelvin is the SI base unit for temperature, starting at absolute zero (−273.15°C) — the theoretical lowest possible temperature where all molecular motion stops. Rankine is an absolute scale like Kelvin but uses Fahrenheit-sized degrees; it's primarily used in some US engineering applications.
